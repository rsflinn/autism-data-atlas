# Voice and Style — Autism Data Atlas

A working style guide. Every page on the site follows it. So does every prompt fed to Claude Design or Claude Code when generating Atlas content.

## The overriding principle

Accuracy beats structure. The goal is to be accurate, neutral, and informative. The rules below serve that goal. They are not the goal.

If applying any rule on this page would push a writer toward invention, padding, or generic filler — leave the rule. Drop a paragraph. Trim the lead. Skip the structure entirely if the verified facts don't support it. A short, true entry is always better than a long, well-shaped one with manufactured connective tissue.

This applies to every section that follows.

## Tone

Wire-service journalism for a general audience. Neutral, direct, plain, active. No marketing softening, no academic bloat. Write the way a Reuters or Bloomberg reporter would write a science brief that has to land equally for a parent, a journalist, a policy aide, and a working researcher.

The reading level target is high-school English. The sentence length target is short — most sentences under 25 words. The paragraph length target is short — three to five sentences.

## Five rules

1. **Lead with the so-what.** First sentence of any page, card, or summary names the subject and tells the reader why it matters. No buildup, no scene-setting.

2. **Define every technical term in plain language on first use.** Once defined, the term can be used again. Examples: "the gene SCN2A, which helps brain cells send signals" — first use. Subsequent uses: "the SCN2A gene." If the term isn't load-bearing, drop it entirely and use the plain phrase.

3. **Avoid gene names, accession codes, and acronyms unless they are the news.** A reader who came to learn about autism shouldn't have to learn what SHANK3, ChromBPNet, or CRISPRi means before they can understand a sentence. If a gene name is essential, it goes after the plain-language phrase ("a gene that builds connections between brain cells, called SHANK3").

4. **Active voice. Nouns and verbs over adjectives and adverbs.** "Researchers found a pattern" beats "It was discovered that there appears to be a pattern." "The study tested 50,000 people" beats "An exceptionally large-scale study was conducted."

5. **Show with facts, not intensifiers.** Numbers, dates, sample sizes, places, names. Banned words: groundbreaking, revolutionary, novel, cutting-edge, paradigm-shifting, interesting, importantly, notably, candidly, frankly. If the fact is significant, the fact says so on its own.

## Structure: the three-paragraph lead

Every wiki entry, gene page, dataset description, and explainer follows this Bloomberg-derived structure for the first 100–200 words. Anything longer goes below it as optional reading. Use the structure when the verified facts support it. Skip a paragraph or shorten the lead when they don't — never invent material to fill the slot.

**Paragraph 1 — Theme.** *What + so-what.* One sentence on what the page is about. One sentence on why a reader should care. Reader can stop here and have the headline.

**Paragraph 2 — Details.** The supporting facts. Sample sizes, dates, regions, numbers, places. Citable evidence drawn from the source. No interpretation.

**Paragraph 3 — Nut graf.** What's at stake in the bigger picture. How this fact connects to other findings, other genes, other datasets on the Atlas. The reader who started cold should now know why this entry deserves a place on the site.

This is a Bloomberg news lead minus the quotation paragraph. Quotes are a reporting tool for stories where the journalist talked to a source. The Atlas indexes published research — the citation does the work a quote would do.

## When a quote is appropriate

Most pages will not need one. The Atlas summarizes published research, and the citation does the work. If a quote does belong on a page — for example, when explaining the motivation behind a project, or when a study author has said something on the public record that adds something the paper itself doesn't — these rules apply.

Verify every quote against the source. Use the quote in its own paragraph. Use only `said` for attribution — never `noted`, `emphasized`, `argued`, `claimed`, `highlighted`. Don't assign emotion (`worried`, `excited`). Identify the speaker on first attribution with role and affiliation in plain English. Never invent or paraphrase-into-quotes. If you can't find a real, sourced statement that adds something, leave the page without a quote.

## Punctuation and formatting

No Oxford comma. No em-dashes inside sentences except where a parenthetical phrase is unavoidable; prefer a comma or a sentence break. No curly quotes — straight only. No emoji. No bullets inside prose. Don't bold for emphasis inside paragraphs. Use bullets only for genuine lists where order doesn't matter.

## What never appears on the site

- Promotional language about the Atlas itself
- The author's name in headlines, hero text, or page bodies
- Disclaimers about Claude or AI
- "Honesty theater" — phrases that announce honesty rather than embody it
- Vague attributions ("experts say", "scientists believe")
- Claims that aren't traceable to a citation visible on the page
