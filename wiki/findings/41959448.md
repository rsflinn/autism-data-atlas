# Distinct cellular DNA methylation mechanisms underlie common and rare genetic risk for brain disorders

## Key Findings
Deep learning framework predicts DNA methylation from sequence across 186 brain cell subtypes, distinguishing CG methylation (mCG) from neuron-enriched non-CG methylation (mCH). Common variants affecting mCG in excitatory neurons show strong heritability enrichment for brain traits. De novo noncoding mutations in autism preferentially perturb mCH -- not mCG -- at conserved neuronal regulatory regions. This pattern replicated across two independent cohorts totaling 5,782 ASD probands and 4,053 unaffected siblings.

## Relevance to Atlas
Directly addresses the central question of how common vs. rare noncoding variants contribute to autism through distinct regulatory mechanisms. The mCG/mCH separation provides a new mechanistic framework: common risk acts through CG methylation in excitatory neurons, while rare de novo noncoding mutations act through non-CG methylation at conserved sites. Cell-type resolution across 186 subtypes is among the most granular available.

## Genes
SCN2A, GRIN2B (among broader NDD gene sets tested)

## Methods
Deep learning (sequence-to-methylation prediction), single-nucleus DNAm profiles from 46 brain regions, heritability enrichment (S-LDSC), de novo mutation burden analysis, replication across two ASD cohorts.
