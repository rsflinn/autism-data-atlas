# The state of autism genetic research, 2004 to 2026

The Autism Data Atlas now indexes 953 papers spanning 22 years of research into the genetic and biological roots of autism. The corpus shows a field that has shifted from cataloguing rare gene mutations one at a time to mapping how dozens of genes converge on shared brain pathways and how common DNA variation contributes to liability across the population. It also shows where the field has not gone: into the lives of profoundly affected adults, into the regulatory DNA outside genes, into the variants that escape standard diagnostic tests, and into clinical trials of any drug that targets the underlying biology.

What follows is a description of the corpus drawn from its own contents. Every claim is tied to papers indexed in the Atlas. The corpus is not a complete census of autism research, but it is a representative cross-section pulled from PubMed and bioRxiv across the queries that built the Atlas.

## The shape of the corpus

The 953 papers span publication years 2004 to 2026, with publication volume rising sharply after 2015. Annual counts in the corpus are about a dozen in the early years, climb past 50 by 2019, and reach 123 papers from 2024 alone. The 2022 to 2024 window contributes 307 papers, roughly a third of the entire archive. The 2025 and 2026 entries are partial and reflect what was already published or available as preprints when the corpus was built.

The corpus is dominated by the peer-reviewed literature. PubMed contributes 856 papers, with another 58 from earlier weekly scans of the live PubMed feed. Preprints from bioRxiv and medRxiv account for 34 papers, a small fraction. Nine of the ten most-represented journals are clinical or molecular biology titles, including Molecular Psychiatry, Molecular Autism, Translational Psychiatry, Cell Reports and Biological Psychiatry. Nature, Nature Communications and Cell appear too, but at lower counts.

The volume curve tracks the sequencing era. The Simons Simplex Collection began publishing exome studies in 2011 to 2012. The Autism Sequencing Consortium followed. Whole-genome sequencing of autism families became routine after about 2017. Single-cell brain sequencing scaled in the same window. The Atlas reflects a field that has been doubling roughly every five years in the volume of work it produces, driven by falling sequencing costs and the expansion of cohorts like SPARK and MSSNG.

## Genes that recur across decades

A small number of genes carry the field on their backs. The corpus includes 28 papers about CHD8, 28 about MECP2, 22 about SHANK3, 19 about SCN2A and 18 about SYNGAP1. After that, FMR1 (the fragile X gene) appears 16 times, TSC2 fifteen, TSC1 thirteen, and the chromatin remodeling gene ARID1B and the cancer gene PTEN ten times each. The full top tier looks like a roster of monogenic syndromes that include autism as a feature: Rett, Phelan-McDermid, Cowden, tuberous sclerosis, fragile X, MEF2C and ADNP haploinsufficiency.

Each of these genes followed a similar arc. A clinical syndrome was named, often in the 1990s or earlier. The gene was identified, often in the 2000s. Mouse models followed in the late 2000s. Patient-derived stem cells followed in the 2010s. Brain organoid studies began appearing in the late 2010s. By the 2020s, the field returned to these same genes with single-cell sequencing, looking for the cell-type specificity it had missed before. The Atlas captures all of those waves stacked on the same gene name.

What the gene list does not show is much movement at the long tail. Hundreds of other genes have been linked to autism through large-scale exome and whole-genome sequencing, but most appear in the corpus only once or twice. The field publishes intensively about a relatively small set of named-syndrome genes and lightly about the broader risk gene catalog. That asymmetry is itself a finding.

The DOIs that anchor this concentration are in the corpus. Examples include Lipton and colleagues on MEF2C organoids (10.1038/s41380-024-02761-9), Geschwind and Pasca on patient-derived organoids (10.1038/s41586-025-10047-5), and the Jin laboratory's mouse Perturb-seq survey of 35 risk genes (10.1126/science.aaz6063).

## Where the field has converged

Three findings replicate across the corpus and across decades.

The first is that autism risk genes converge on synaptic biology. Papers from the early 2010s onward describe disrupted glutamate signaling, altered ion channels, and abnormal synaptic protein scaffolds in models of FMR1 loss, SHANK3 loss, MECP2 loss, TSC1 and TSC2 loss, and CHD8 loss. The 22 SHANK3 papers in the corpus are almost entirely about synaptic structure and plasticity. The convergence claim is not new; it appears in review articles dated 2010, 2015, 2019 and 2024 in the Atlas.

The second is that autism genes are highly enriched in transcriptional regulators and chromatin modifiers. The corpus contains many papers on this theme, including those on CHD8, ARID1B, KMT2C, MECP2, EP300 and the broader BAF complex. The Paulsen 2023 CHOOSE screen perturbed 36 such genes in human organoids and found shared downstream effects on synapse and ion channel programs (10.1038/s41586-023-06473-y). The 2026 Pasca and Geschwind paper extends the same logic to nine patient-derived models (10.1038/s41586-025-10047-5).

The third area of convergence is between the genetics of autism and the genetics of epilepsy. About 30 percent of children with autism develop epilepsy. The corpus contains many papers on the genes that sit at this intersection, including SCN2A, SCN1A, KCNB1, GRIN2A and PCDH19. The Epi25 collaboration published the largest such study to date in 2024, and the cross-condition signal is one of the most reproducible patterns in the corpus.

## Where the field has diverged

Convergence has limits. The 2026 Pasca and Geschwind paper compared organoids carrying nine different autism-linked mutations and found that in early development each mutation produced a distinct molecular profile. The mutations only began to look alike at the latest time points. Convergence appears at the pathway level. It does not appear at the level of individual genes or specific cell states.

The Atlas also contains an explicit failure of bottleneck-gene replication. The Paulsen CHOOSE screen identified specific downstream genes as convergent targets across many autism gene knockouts. Two independent datasets in the corpus, the 2026 Pasca human organoid study and the Jin laboratory's 2020 mouse in vivo study, did not reproduce that specific gene list. The phenomenon of convergence is real across all three datasets. The exact downstream genes that converge depend on the system. That distinction is now visible across the literature in a way it was not five years ago.

There is also a tension between rare-variant and common-variant work. Studies of named-syndrome genes dominate the corpus by paper count. Studies of common variants, captured in the 16 papers in the corpus that explicitly use the terms polygenic, polygenic risk score, or genome-wide association study in their abstracts, are a much smaller share. Yet the published heritability literature, including Gaugler 2014, attributes about 49 percent of variance in autism liability to common inherited variants. The corpus underrepresents that mechanism because the search queries that built it lean toward gene-by-gene mechanism studies. The next backfill cycle should weight more toward GWAS, expression quantitative trait loci, and population-scale studies.

## Trends in study type

The most visible trend in the corpus is the rise of brain organoid and induced pluripotent stem cell research. Papers using these tools begin to appear in 2014 and 2015, climb steadily through the late 2010s, and now form a substantial share of the most recent year of the corpus. Single-cell RNA sequencing tracks a similar arc starting around 2018. Spatial transcriptomics papers begin to appear in 2022 and 2023. The Atlas includes work that combines these tools, including the 2026 Geschwind paper and the 2026 Vuong et al. study of Down syndrome cortex (10.1126/science.aea1259).

Mouse model work remains a steady backbone of the corpus. Papers on Mecp2 knockouts, Fmr1 knockouts, Shank3 knockouts, Tsc1 and Tsc2 knockouts, and Cntnap2 knockouts appear across all years. The technology stack changes over time. Behavior phenotyping in 2010 gives way to electrophysiology in 2014, single-cell sequencing in 2020, and integrated multiomic analysis in 2024. The same gene pulls in new tools as the tools become available.

Computational and theoretical work makes up a meaningful share, especially in the most recent years. The corpus contains papers on machine learning models of regulatory variant effect, polygenic prediction methods, and integration of single-cell data with genome-wide association results. These papers have grown faster than experimental work in the last three years.

Clinical trial papers are scarce. The corpus contains a handful of intervention studies, mostly for fragile X, tuberous sclerosis or behavioral therapy. Trials of pharmacological agents specifically targeting the synaptic or chromatin biology described in the mechanism literature are rare. The mGluR5 antagonist trials of the early 2010s, which targeted fragile X synaptic biology, are present, including a 2010 Pharmacology and Therapeutics review on those efforts (10.1016/j.pharmthera.2010.02.008). Most of those trials failed in clinical endpoints, and the field has not produced a successor wave at scale.

## Gaps and quiet areas

Several gaps stand out from a parent's-eye view of the corpus.

Profoundly affected adults are largely absent. The corpus contains many papers on early childhood autism diagnosis, on language, and on cognitive testing in children. Studies that follow severely affected nonverbal individuals into adulthood, characterize their genetic profiles in detail, or describe their long-term outcomes are rare. Search terms like "profound autism", "nonverbal" and "severe" returned a relatively small subset of papers, and most of those are on outcome measurement rather than mechanism.

Idiopathic, undiagnosed cases like the patient who motivates this Atlas are also underrepresented. Most genetic studies in the corpus describe individuals with known mutations. Studies that take a large cohort of clinically severe but genetically negative cases and pursue noncoding, structural, or polygenic explanations are uncommon. The 2026 Pasca and Geschwind organoid paper includes eight idiopathic cell lines and is one of the most informative entries in the corpus on this point. There are not many like it.

Regulatory DNA outside genes is studied, but unevenly. Papers explicitly using terms like enhancer, promoter, MPRA or massively parallel reporter assay are present in the corpus, including the 2024 IRF6 oral cleft work (10.1101/2024.06.01.596914), but the count is small relative to coding-variant studies. The standard clinical exome captures only a small slice of the genome. The gap between what is sequenced clinically and what is studied in noncoding research labs remains wide.

Pharmacological trials targeting the molecular biology in the mechanism literature are unusually scarce given the volume of mechanism work. Many of the most-cited mouse studies in the corpus end with a sentence about therapeutic implications. Few of those implications make it to a clinical trial. The corpus suggests a field that produces target hypotheses faster than it produces interventions.

Adult brain tissue from people with severe, undiagnosed autism is the most acute gap. Postmortem brain studies in the corpus rely heavily on a small number of brain banks. Coverage of severely affected adults, especially nonverbal adults, is thin. Most large datasets are biased toward less-affected cases who could consent and participate.

## What is likely to land next

Three signals in the corpus point at near-term direction.

The first is that single-cell and spatial multiomics will keep dominating new papers. The number of corpus entries in 2024 to 2026 that combine single-nucleus RNA, ATAC, and spatial methods is rising fast. The 2026 Vuong et al. Down syndrome paper, the 2026 Geschwind organoid paper, and the 2026 Sanders and Nowakowski BA22 paper all use this stack. Smaller labs have started publishing in the same vein. The corpus suggests that within two years, integrated multiomic profiles will be standard for any new autism risk gene paper.

The second is a return to common variants. The Sanders and Nowakowski work in the user's existing notes, the new MPRA papers, and recent eQTL work in fetal brain tissue all suggest the field is rebuilding its common-variant infrastructure with cell-type resolution. The next wave of GWAS-derived findings is likely to be cell-type and developmental-stage specific, not gene-by-gene.

The third is convergence between human genetic and AI-driven regulatory variant scoring. Papers in the corpus from 2024 to 2026 increasingly reference deep learning models of gene regulation, including ChromBPNet, Enformer, AlphaGenome and successor systems. These tools let researchers ask whether a noncoding variant in a single patient is likely to disrupt a regulatory element that matters in a specific cell type. The Atlas's own ChromBPNet work fits in that wave. More such studies are likely.

Clinical translation remains the slowest layer. Papers on antisense oligonucleotides for individual rare-variant cases (Mila-1, n-of-1 ASOs for severe pediatric disease) are present in the corpus and growing, but these are bespoke programs, not platform therapies. The corpus does not contain credible evidence of a near-term general autism drug. The most realistic forecast is that mechanism-targeted ASO and small-molecule trials will arrive gene by gene, starting with the syndromes that already have natural-history infrastructure: SCN2A, SYNGAP1, MECP2, SHANK3 and CDKL5.

The Atlas was built from these papers, and these papers were built on cohorts and methods that the Atlas also catalogs. What it will tell parents, journalists, and researchers a year from now depends on whether the next backfill closes the gaps documented here, especially on adults, idiopathic cases, regulatory DNA, and treatment.
