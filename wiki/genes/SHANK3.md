# SHANK3

## Overview

SHANK3 (SH3 and Multiple Ankyrin Repeat Domains 3) is a postsynaptic scaffolding protein whose loss of function causes Phelan-McDermid syndrome (PMS), a rare neurodevelopmental disorder with nearly 80% of affected individuals meeting diagnostic criteria for autism spectrum disorder. Deletions or loss-of-function mutations in SHANK3 are identified in approximately 1% of autism spectrum disorder cases, making it one of the most common single-gene causes of autism.

## Role and Function

**Postsynaptic Scaffolding:** SHANK3 encodes a multi-domain protein that forms the structural scaffold at the postsynaptic density (PSD), the electron-dense specialization across from the presynaptic neurotransmitter release machinery.

**Glutamate Receptor Organization:** SHANK3 organizes glutamate receptors and associated signaling proteins at excitatory synapses through its multiple protein-interaction domains (SH3 and ankyrin repeats).

**Synapse Development:** Essential for proper maturation and function of excitatory synapses; regulates dendritic spine morphology and stability.

**Synaptic Plasticity:** Required for long-term potentiation (LTP) and other forms of synaptic plasticity underlying learning and memory.

## Key Findings

### Phelan-McDermid Syndrome: High Autism Penetrance

**Finding:** SHANK3 deletions cause Phelan-McDermid syndrome, characterized by neonatal hypotonia, absent to severely delayed speech, developmental delay, and minor dysmorphic facial features.

**Autism Prevalence:** Nearly 80% of Phelan-McDermid syndrome patients are diagnosed with autism spectrum disorder.

**Clinical Features:**
- Neonatal hypotonia (initial sign)
- Absent to severely delayed speech
- Global developmental delay
- Autism spectrum disorder (core feature in majority)
- Minor dysmorphic facial features
- Intellectual disability (variable severity)

### SHANK3 Mutations in Autism Without Syndromic Features

**Finding:** Beyond classic Phelan-McDermid syndrome deletions, point mutations and smaller deletions in SHANK3 are identified in approximately 1% of autism spectrum disorder cases without classic syndromic features.

**Spectrum:** SHANK3 mutations show phenotypic heterogeneity, ranging from classic severe PMS to milder autism with specific social and synaptic deficits.

### Synaptic Mechanisms: HCN Channel Channelopathy

**Finding:** SHANK3-deficient neurons exhibit impaired postsynaptic HCN (hyperpolarization-activated cyclic nucleotide-gated) channel function, leading to reduced dendritic integration of synaptic signals.

**Mechanism:**
- SHANK3 loss reduces HCN channel expression and localization
- HCN channels regulate neuronal excitability and dendritic integration
- Impaired HCN function reduces neuronal responsiveness to synaptic inputs
- Results in reduced synaptic efficacy despite normal glutamate release

**Mouse Model Evidence:** Shank3 knockout mice show autism-like behavioral phenotypes (reduced social interaction, altered repetitive behaviors) accompanied by electrophysiological deficits in synaptic transmission.

## Autism Relevance

SHANK3 represents a major structural component of the excitatory synapse whose disruption directly impairs social cognitive circuits. The high penetrance of autism in Phelan-McDermid syndrome (80% of cases) combined with the identification of SHANK3 mutations in 1% of autism cases makes it one of the most autism-relevant single genes. The mechanism involves postsynaptic dysfunction affecting glutamate receptor signaling, consistent with convergence models of autism pathophysiology.

**Evidence Tier: B** -- Real synaptic mechanism in model systems; autism association confirmed in multiple cohorts but not independently replicated in second-line perturbation screens (CHOOSE screen did not test SHANK3 perturbation).

## Receptor Separation Relevance

SHANK3 is a structural component of the postsynaptic density at glutamate synapses. Loss of SHANK3 specifically impairs glutamate receptor organization and signaling without direct effects on GABAergic synapses. Classification: **glutamate** (direct glutamate synapse scaffolding function).

## Noncoding Regulatory Context

Phelan-McDermid syndrome is predominantly caused by 22q13 terminal deletions removing SHANK3 completely, or by intragenic SHANK3 deletions and point mutations. Cis-regulatory variants affecting SHANK3 expression have not been systematically characterized. The terminal deletions at 22q13.33 also remove neighboring genes (RABL2B, SULT4A1, among others), making it difficult to assign clinical features solely to SHANK3 haploinsufficiency in deletion cases.
