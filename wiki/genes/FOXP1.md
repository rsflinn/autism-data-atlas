# FOXP1

## Overview

FOXP1 (Forkhead Box P1) is a transcription factor whose loss of function causes FOXP1 syndrome, a neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by autism, intellectual disability, and speech and language impairment. Over 200 individuals with FOXP1 variants have been identified, making it a well-characterized cause of syndromic autism.

## Role and Function

**Transcriptional Regulation:** FOXP1 encodes a member of the forkhead family of transcriptional repressors that regulates gene expression from early development through adulthood.

**Tissue Expression:** Widely expressed across the brain, heart, lung, esophagus, immune system, and spinal motor neurons.

**Regulatory Role:** Functions as a transcriptional repressor affecting developmental programs in neurons and neural progenitors.

## Key Findings

### De Novo Loss-of-Function Variants in Autism

**Finding:** De novo loss-of-function variants in FOXP1 were identified in male patients with autism, intellectual disability, and severe speech and language impairment, including cases from the Simons Simplex Collection.

**Phenotype:** All nine initially characterized individuals with FOXP1 variants presented with autism spectrum disorder, with additional common features including intellectual disability, hypotonia, dysarthria, and fine/gross motor coordination deficits.

### FOXP1 Syndrome Phenotype

**Core Features:**
- Mild-to-severe intellectual disability
- Speech and language impairment (universal feature)
- Autism spectrum disorder or autistic features
- Attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)
- Anxiety
- Repetitive behaviors
- Sensory symptoms
- Dysmorphic features

**Prevalence:** Over 200 cases identified to date; de novo dominant inheritance is the predominant mode of inheritance, though rare familial cases exist.

## Autism Relevance

FOXP1 represents a clear monogenic cause of syndromic autism with predictable phenotype. The consistent presentation across patients suggests a core neurodevelopmental role. Loss of FOXP1 function disrupts transcriptional programs critical for normal social development, language acquisition, and cognitive development.

**Evidence Tier: B** -- Multiple independent cases confirm de novo FOXP1 variants as disease-causing, but dataset-dependent downstream effects (CHOOSE screen shows no ORC-specific dysregulation from FOXP1 knockout compared to MEF2C's specific effects).

## Receptor Separation Relevance

FOXP1 perturbations do not produce selective glutamate or GABA receptor dysregulation in CHOOSE multiplexed screen. FOXP1 knockout showed minimal downstream effects compared to other autism gene perturbations, suggesting FOXP1-specific mechanism rather than convergence on ion channel pathways. Classification: **shared** (affects both excitatory and inhibitory development but without specific receptor-type bias).

## Noncoding Regulatory Context

Current literature emphasizes coding de novo loss-of-function variants as primary mechanism. Regulatory variants in FOXP1 cis-elements have not been systematically characterized. The gene occupies regulatory regions that control developmental programs, but specific enhancer variants have not been identified in large-scale studies.

## Key Datasets

- Simons Simplex Collection -- Initial identification of de novo variants
- SPARK -- Autism patient cohorts with FOXP1 variants
- International FOXP1 Foundation cohort -- Largest phenotypic characterization (200+ cases)
- CHOOSE -- Multiplexed perturbation screen showing FOXP1 knockout produces minimal convergent effects
