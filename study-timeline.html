When Genes Turn On in the Developing Brain
Postmortem Brain ▼
The Study
BrainSpan maps the developmental transcriptome from 8 weeks post-conception through 40 years. Shows exactly when and where genes turn on and off during critical periods of brain formation.
Why It Matters
Establishes the normal timeline of gene activity — essential context for understanding when autism-associated genes are active during critical developmental windows.
The Limitation
Shows normal development. Doesn't directly identify what goes wrong in autism, but provides critical baseline for comparison.

The Hidden Switches That Control Our Genes
Computational ▼
The Study
ENCODE Phase 1 produced genome-wide maps of transcription factor binding sites, histone modifications, and chromatin accessibility across hundreds of cell types. Created the foundation for understanding DNA regulatory architecture.
Why It Matters
Non-coding variants that disrupt transcription factor binding sites are invisible to gene-centric analyses. ENCODE maps gave us the reference to predict which variants affect regulation.
The Limitation
Describes potential regulatory sites. Doesn't explain causation or prioritize which are functionally important in brain.

What's Normal? A Genetic Reference for Everyone
Genetic Cohort ▼
The Study
gnomAD aggregated exome and genome sequencing from 60K+ individuals, creating population-level allele frequency data for every possible coding variant. Became the reference standard for filtering variants.
Why It Matters
Can't distinguish disease variants from common benign variation without knowing population frequencies. gnomAD made it possible to confidently identify rare variants that don't appear in controls.
The Limitation
Coding variants only in original data. Regulatory variants require different frequency databases. Doesn't explain what makes a variant pathogenic.

Which Genes Are Active in Which Body Parts
Postmortem Brain ▼
The Study
GTEx surveyed gene expression across 26 brain regions and 48 total tissue types from 900+ individuals. Revealed that most genes show tissue-specific and brain-region-specific expression patterns.
Why It Matters
Autism-relevant genes don't all express in cortex. Some are specific to striatum, amygdala, or other circuits. GTEx provides the reference for interpreting which regulatory changes matter in brain specifically.
The Limitation
Adult postmortem tissues. Doesn't capture developmental stage-specific expression or cell-type-specific patterns within regions.

The Definitive List of Autism-Linked Genes
Genetic Cohort ▼
The Study
SFARI Base aggregated published autism association data, creating a curated gene list ranked by evidence strength. Started with hundreds of candidate genes and methodically sorted by experimental support.
Why It Matters
Not all "autism genes" are equal. SFARI provided a structured way to distinguish high-confidence genes (de novo LoF recurrence, functional validation) from speculative associations.
The Limitation
Only published data. Small de novo sample sizes mean many real genes may not yet reach genome-wide significance. Limited functional validation.

Autism and Schizophrenia Share Molecular Roots
Postmortem Brain ▼
The Study
Researchers analyzed RNA-seq from cortical and subcortical brain regions in autism vs. controls, discovering that different mutations converge on overlapping transcriptional disruptions despite being caused by different genes.
Why It Matters
Different genetic causes can produce similar downstream molecular phenotypes. This supports a convergence model where many genes affect shared pathways, explaining why autism appears clinically similar despite heterogeneous genetic origins.
The Limitation
Adult postmortem tissue. Can't determine if convergence is primary (direct common pathway) or secondary (compensation/downstream effects). No functional validation of disrupted genes.

A Clinical Dictionary for Genetic Variants
Computational ▼
The Study
ClinVar aggregates expert-curated clinical interpretations of genetic variants. Laboratories worldwide contribute evidence of pathogenicity or benignity, building a community database of variant classification.
Why It Matters
Single variants sometimes have conflicting interpretations. ClinVar metadata shows which genes have strong clinical evidence and which remain speculative. Essential for filtering Teagan's VCF.
The Limitation
Only coding variants in scope. Pathogenicity interpretations are delayed (most interpretation happens after papers publish). Many rare variants remain unclassified.

Autism Variants Target Specific Brain Cell Types
Postmortem Brain ▼
The Study
Researchers produced the most comprehensive map of chromatin state across 8 human brain cell types (excitatory neurons, inhibitory neurons, astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, etc.). Showed that chromatin accessibility varies dramatically by cell type.
Why It Matters
A regulatory variant in a GABAergic inhibitory neuron enhancer won't affect glutamatergic neurons. Nott's cell-type-specific chromatin map made it possible to predict which cell types are affected by which variants.
The Limitation
Adult brain only. Regulatory landscape changes dramatically during development. Early developmental enhancers in iPSCs or organoids may differ from adult.

Knocking Out Autism Genes in Living Mouse Brains
Mouse Model ▼
The Study
Researchers performed in vivo CRISPR perturbation of 35 autism risk genes in developing mouse neocortex at E12.5, then profiled with scRNA-seq at P7. First systematic functional test of whether these genes affect neural development.
Why It Matters
Genetic association doesn't prove function. Jin's perturb-seq showed which genes actually disrupt neuronal identity, migration, or differentiation. Started moving beyond "this gene is associated" to "this gene does this."
The Limitation
Mouse cortex, complete knockout, early developmental stage. Single-gene perturbations don't model polygenic regulatory burden. Adult human brain may differ substantially.

Knocking Out 10,000 Genes to See What Breaks
Mouse Model ▼
The Study
Researchers developed pooled CRISPR screening coupled to scRNA-seq (Perturb-seq), allowing thousands of single-gene knockouts to be profiled simultaneously in K562 cells. Major technological advance in functional genomics.
Why It Matters
Single perturbations are labor-intensive. Perturb-seq enabled genome-scale screening with single-cell resolution. Applied to autism genes, it showed which genes affect common downstream pathways.
The Limitation
K562 cells are immortalized cancer cells, not brain. Cell-line-specific effects may not translate to neurons. Doesn't capture cell-type-specific or developmental-stage-specific effects.

36 Autism Genes Knocked Out in Mini-Brains
Brain Organoid ▼
The Study
Researchers created mosaic cerebral organoids with CRISPR knockouts of 36 autism genes, performed scRNA-seq to profile effects on neuronal differentiation across excitatory and inhibitory lineages.
Why It Matters
First systematic screen of autism genes in human-derived organoid development. Moves beyond mouse models to human developmental biology. Shows which genes affect which neuronal lineages.
The Limitation
Fetal-equivalent stage. Mosaic design (one KO per cell) has less power for cell-non-autonomous effects. No environmental/genetic background variation from real patients.

Different Receptor Types Separate Autism from Epilepsy
Genetic Cohort ▼
The Study
Epi25 Collaborative exome sequenced ~21K epilepsy cases and 33K controls, identified genes with significant rare variant burden in epilepsy. Separated genes by phenotype (DEE, GGE, focal epilepsy).
Why It Matters
Not all epilepsy genes cause autism. Epi25 revealed that GABA receptors carry high burden in epilepsy but are rarely disrupted in pure autism GWAS. Suggests distinct genetic architectures for autism vs. epilepsy despite phenotypic overlap.
The Limitation
Phenotypes are broad (all epilepsy types lumped). Rare variants might be clinically actionable but genetically heterogeneous. Doesn't explain mechanism.

Ion Channels Go Quiet in Autistic Brains
Postmortem Brain ▼
The Study
Researchers performed single-cell RNA-seq on postmortem cortex from autism and control brains, revealing that transcriptomic disruptions in autism are cell-type-specific — some cell types show strong signatures while others are largely spared.
Why It Matters
Autism isn't a brain-wide disruption — it's cell-type-selective. Excitatory neurons and some interneuron populations show stronger convergence than others. Points toward specific circuits being disrupted.
The Limitation
Pooled data across genotypes. Can't attribute specific transcriptomic changes to specific mutations. Doesn't distinguish primary effects from compensation.

The Largest Study of Lab-Grown Autism Brain Tissue
Brain Organoid ▼
The Study
Researchers generated hiPS cell-derived organoids from 70 ASD patients (55 individuals) carrying rare mutations, plus idiopathic ASD cases. Identified convergent transcriptional programs despite genetic heterogeneity.
Why It Matters
First large-scale study of real patient mutations in human organoids at scale. Shows early developmental convergence: different mutations cause similar patterns of dysregulation by mid-organoid development.
The Limitation
Fetal-stage organoids only. Postnatal brain may diverge. Idiopathic cases (no known mutation) produce weak signals. Some mutations may require postnatal environment to show effects.

When One Gene Fails, Others Try to Compensate
Clinical Data ▼
The Study
Researchers studied patient-derived iPSC organoids from MEF2C haploinsufficiency cases, discovered that MEF2C loss specifically dysregulates autism-linked transcription factors (FOXP1, TCF4, MYT1L) with compensatory upregulation in neurons.
Why It Matters
MEF2C sits at a topological convergence point where its loss reverberates through the regulatory network. Shows network causation: one hub gene disruption affects multiple downstream partners, not just direct targets.
The Limitation
Single monogenic model (uniform 50% loss). Doesn't test partial regulatory reductions from polygenic burden. Effect may not generalize to other autism genes.

Updated Maps of Gene Control Regions
Postmortem Brain ▼
The Study
Roadmap Epigenomics updated chromatin maps across 21 brain regions with modern sequencing technology (ATAC-seq, ChIP-seq), achieving higher resolution and sensitivity than earlier Phase 1 data.
Why It Matters
Regulatory landscape maps improve over time as sequencing deepens. Updated Roadmap data enables better prediction of which non-coding variants affect brain-specific regulatory elements across multiple regions.
The Limitation
Adult tissue only. Developmental chromatin patterns differ. Region-averaged samples mask cell-type-specific effects within regions.

Machine Learning Predicts Which Variants Matter
Computational ▼
The Study
ChromBPNet scored 42K+ SNVs across autism-linked regulatory genes in brain cell types using deep learning trained on ATAC-seq and ChIP-seq data. Predicted which variants most likely disrupt transcription factor binding in specific cell types.
Why It Matters
Regulatory variant prediction has moved from heuristics to machine learning. ChromBPNet enables genome-scale prioritization of variants by predicted functional impact in brain-specific context.
The Limitation
Predictions, not validation. Top-scoring variants are overwhelmingly common polymorphisms. Lack independent functional validation. Cell-type predictions don't capture developmental dynamics.
